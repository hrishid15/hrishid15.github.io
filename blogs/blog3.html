A Leap of Faith

December 27, 2025

My dad recently sent a video into our family group chat, as most dads do when they find something that they believe is so profound or interesting that they have to share it with everyone. Usually, I tend to neglect these videos (sorry Dad if you’re reading this) because I’m not always interested in “Why the World Feels More Hateful & Distrustful” or “The World’s Nicest Motorhome” — which, by the way, are real articles he has sent us before. This time, however, in a rare moment of filial duty, I decided to watch the video, which turned out to be a debate between an atheist and a religious scholar regarding the existence of God (or a ‘supernatural power’, whatever floats your boat). About 15 minutes into this debate, the atheist asked the scholar a question: what is the difference between faith and belief? It was at this point that I paused the video in confusion, as I’d always thought both of those were the same thing. We toss around the phrases “have faith” and “just believe” so easily these days that I’d never thought to consider how faith and belief could actually be two different concepts. Instead of continuing to watch (which would have probably given me some answers) I decided to take my own shot at deciphering the difference between them. After a lot of pondering and several conversations with my dad, I was able to come to a conclusion that I think is pretty satisfying.

What's the Difference?

Imagine standing in front of a bridge that you’ve never crossed before. Engineers have certified this bridge as structurally sound, lots of other people cross it daily without issue, and overall it looks solid. Based on all this evidence, you believe that the bridge is safe — accepting the idea that the bridge is safe to be a fact. But having faith in that bridge is something else entirely. Faith is actually walking across it. It's putting your weight on it, trusting it with your safety, committing to the act of crossing despite any lingering uncertainty. That's the initial distinction: belief is intellectual acceptance ("this bridge is safe"), while faith is active commitment ("I'm walking across it").

At first glance, this makes faith seem dependent on belief. After all, you develop faith in the bridge based on your beliefs about its structural integrity. Belief comes first, then faith follows as a natural consequence. But here's where it gets more complicated: faith can also work in reverse, generating belief even when the evidence points the other way. Consider a parent whose child has gone missing. As time passes, the evidence increasingly suggests the worst. Every rational indicator says the child is gone. Yet the parent insists, "I have faith my child is alive and well." In this case, the parent isn't arriving at faith through belief — they don't believe their child is fine simply based on the evidence. Instead, their faith — their refusal to give up and their commitment to hope — is actually creating belief. The act of maintaining faith generates a conviction that defies the facts in front of them. So we now know that faith and belief can flow in both directions. Sometimes belief enables faith (the bridge). Sometimes faith generates belief (the parent). They're not the same thing, but rather interconnected components of something larger.

Dependence on Possibility

Another small nuance about faith that I discovered is that it actually has a boundary condition — it requires openness to possibility. Consider a person who claims, “I don't believe in an afterlife. I think when you die, that's it. But I have faith that I'll see my loved ones again after death.” This definitely seems contradictory, and it is. Although this individual claims to not believe in an afterlife, having faith that they will see their loved ones after death shows that a part of this person is open to the possibility that there could be an afterlife. This possibility that our person houses is what allows them to have that faith in the first place. As a side note, this also shows how humans don’t necessarily have set beliefs, and our claims about our beliefs don’t always align with our actual thoughts. To an extent, we always consider both sides of a dilemma, even when we have a clear favorite.

Circling Back

We now understand that belief is accepting something as true cognitively or intellectually, while faith is a form of commitment or trust driven by possibility in the face of uncertainty. Tying this back to the idea of God and religion that was presented in the debate, I went after what I thought was an interesting question: is it possible to believe in God but not have faith in God? Although this seems contradictory, the answer is pretty simple. Choosing to believe in a supernatural power is just a cognitive action, but you don’t necessarily have to commit to a religion and follow that power or that God. You are simply aware of one another with no actual connection to each other.

So What Now?

At this point you may be asking, “Why does this matter? And what is this larger idea you mentioned earlier that faith and belief are both components of?” And that is pretty much exactly what I asked myself, because I did not know where I was going with this, but it still felt like I was missing something. So I guess my next question to solve would be, what happens when your faith and your belief both align? What’s the end product of that? My immediate answer to this was that faith and belief converge to form a fact, or truth, which is exactly what led me on a tangent regarding discovery vs. invention.

Discovery vs. Invention

The discovery vs. invention debate is one of those philosophical rabbit holes that seems simple at first but gets increasingly complex the deeper you go. When humans create something new — whether it's a piece of technology, a work of art, or a mathematical theorem — are we discovering something that already existed in some abstract space of possibilities, or are we inventing something that never existed before?

Consider Beethoven's Fifth Symphony (I know, its a weird reach). Before Beethoven composed it, did that specific sequence of notes exist anywhere? One perspective, Constructivism, supports that Beethoven created something brand new, bringing a piece of art that had never been there before into existence. The other perspective, Platonism, argues that all possible sequences of musical notes already exist in mathematical and logical space. Therefore, Beethoven didn't create anything, but rather he just found a particularly beautiful arrangement that was always there, waiting to be discovered. Both of these views have merit, but there's something unsatisfying about both extremes of this dilemma. If everything that could possibly exist already "exists" in some abstract mathematical or logical space, doesn't that make the word "exist" basically meaningless?

Platonism vs. Constructivism

Platonism is the philosophical view that abstract objects, concepts, and possibilities exist independently of human minds in some eternal, unchanging realm. This theory is named after Plato, who believed in a "world of Forms" where perfect versions of everything exist. Constructivism is the opposing view that knowledge, concepts, and objects are constructed or created by human minds and societies rather than discovered. Constructivists argue that things don't exist until we actively create them.

This is where the distinction between possibility and actuality becomes really important. Maybe the right answer isn't discovery or invention, but actually something in between. We definitely discover possibilities, whether those are patterns, configurations, or arrangements that could theoretically exist. But then we actualize them — we bring them from the realm of abstract possibility into concrete reality. Beethoven didn't just stumble upon his Fifth Symphony; he had to sit down, commit to the creative process, and transform an abstract possibility into actual music that people could hear.

Full Circle

This is exactly where faith and belief come back into play. Beethoven believed in the mathematical relationships between musical notes, in the patterns that could create beauty, but belief alone doesn't write a symphony. He also needed faith — the commitment to actually compose, the trust in his creative process, the willingness to actualize what he could only imagine. His belief gave him a sense of what was possible, but it was his faith that made it real.

I think that this is the best way to describe what happens when faith and belief align: you become an agent of actualization. You take something from our realm of possibility and transform it into reality. This applies for pretty much anything you could imagine — you believe a goal is possible for you (it exists in the space of things you could achieve), and your faith (commitment and willingness to achieve that goal despite uncertainty) actualizes it. It works for building relationships, pursuing careers, creating art, even engaging with questions of philosophy and meaning (does this count as a 4th wall break?).

Wrapping Up

At this point, it definitely feels like we've deviated from the initial question from the religious debate. But in a sense, I think this deep dive into faith, belief, and their relationship to actualization was fully necessary for understanding why these concepts are so significant to us as humans. Faith and belief aren't just abstract philosophical topics to debate in the context of religion or spirituality. They're fundamental tools that we use on a day-to-day basis to navigate uncertainty and bring new things into existence. Belief maps the territory of what's possible. Faith is what gets us to actually walk the path. And when they work together, aligned in the same direction, that's what allows us to shape the world.

If this seemed interesting to you, I would recommend watching the entire video debate. I did end up finishing it later on and thought that several other interesting points and topics were brought up, but this one stood out to me the most. Thanks for reading and happy holidays!
